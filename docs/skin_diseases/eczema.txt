SKIN DISEASE: ECZEMA (ATOPIC DERMATITIS)
==========================================

OVERVIEW:
Atopic dermatitis (AD) is a chronic, relapsing-remitting inflammatory skin condition characterized by intense pruritus, xerosis, and eczematous lesions. It affects 15-20% of children and 1-3% of adults worldwide. AD is part of the "atopic march" — often preceding allergic rhinitis and asthma development.

CLASSIFICATION:
- Infantile (0-2 years): Predominantly facial and extensor involvement
- Childhood (2-12 years): Flexural distribution, lichenification
- Adolescent/Adult (>12 years): Flexural, hand/foot involvement, chronic lichenified plaques
- Severity: Mild (EASI <7), Moderate (EASI 7-21), Severe (EASI >21)

PATHOPHYSIOLOGY:
A complex interaction between:
1. Genetic defects in skin barrier - FLG (filaggrin) gene mutations in 30% of AD patients
2. Immune dysregulation - Th2-skewed response with elevated IL-4, IL-5, IL-13, IgE
3. Microbiome dysbiosis - Staphylococcus aureus colonization in >90% of lesional skin
4. Environmental sensitization - Allergens penetrate defective barrier, triggering immune cascade
5. Neuroinflammation - Substance P and NGF contribute to itch-scratch cycle

SYMPTOMS:
- Intense, persistent pruritus (hallmark symptom - "the itch that rashes")
- Xerosis (dry, rough skin)
- Erythematous papules, vesicles, and plaques
- Weeping/oozing in acute phase
- Crusting and scaling
- Lichenification (skin thickening) in chronic disease
- Excoriations from scratching
- Dennie-Morgan fold (extra crease under eyes)
- Periorbital darkening

AFFECTED AREAS BY AGE:
- Infants: Cheeks, scalp, extensor surfaces; napkin area typically spared
- Children: Antecubital/popliteal fossae, wrists, ankles, neck
- Adults: Flexural surfaces, hands, eyelids, face, neck, décolletage

TRIGGERS:
- Irritants: soaps, detergents, wool, synthetic fabrics, sweat
- Allergens: house dust mites, pet dander, pollen, mold
- Food allergies (especially in children): milk, egg, wheat, soy, peanuts
- Stress and emotional factors
- Heat and humidity extremes
- Infections (S. aureus, HSV causing eczema herpeticum)
- Hormonal changes

DIAGNOSIS:
Hanifin & Rajka criteria (3 of 4 major + 3 of 23 minor criteria)
Major criteria:
1. Pruritus
2. Typical morphology and distribution
3. Chronic/relapsing course
4. Personal/family history of atopy

Investigations:
- Total serum IgE (elevated in 80%)
- Specific IgE RAST testing
- Patch testing to rule out contact dermatitis
- Skin biopsy if diagnosis uncertain
- Filaggrin gene testing in research settings

TREATMENT PROTOCOLS:

Emollient Therapy (cornerstone):
- Apply within 3 minutes of bathing ("soak and seal")
- Ceramide-containing creams preferred
- 250-500g weekly usage in severe cases

Topical Anti-inflammatory:
- TCS (topical corticosteroids): mild (hydrocortisone 1%) to superpotent (clobetasol 0.05%)
- Topical calcineurin inhibitors: tacrolimus 0.03%/0.1%, pimecrolimus 1% (steroid-sparing)
- Topical PDE4 inhibitor: crisaborole 2% ointment
- Topical JAK inhibitor: ruxolitinib cream 1.5%

Systemic Treatments:
- Dupilumab (IL-4Rα antagonist) - biologic, FDA-approved for moderate-severe AD ≥6 months
- Tralokinumab (IL-13 antagonist) - approved for adults
- JAK inhibitors: abrocitinib, upadacitinib, baricitinib
- Cyclosporine (2.5-5mg/kg/day) - short-term rescue
- Methotrexate, azathioprine, mycophenolate mofetil
- Oral corticosteroids: short courses only (risk of rebound)
- Omalizumab for high-IgE phenotypes

Phototherapy:
- Narrowband UVB (first-line phototherapy)
- Broadband UVB
- UVA1 for acute flares
- PUVA for severe recalcitrant disease

COMPLICATIONS:
- Eczema herpeticum (Kaposi's varicelliform eruption) - herpes simplex superinfection, EMERGENCY
- Secondary bacterial infections (S. aureus, Streptococcus)
- Erythroderma
- Ocular complications: keratoconus, cataracts
- Sleep disturbance, anxiety, depression
- Growth impairment in severe pediatric cases

PREVENTION:
- Early emollient intervention from birth (shown to reduce AD incidence 30-50%)
- Probiotic supplementation in pregnancy/infancy (evidence growing)
- Avoidance of identified triggers
- Wet wrap therapy for acute flares
- Proactive therapy: intermittent TCS/TCI on previously affected areas

ICD-10 CODE: L20.9 (Atopic dermatitis, unspecified)
