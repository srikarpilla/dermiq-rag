SKIN DISEASE: URTICARIA (HIVES)
================================

OVERVIEW:
Urticaria is characterized by transient, intensely pruritic wheals (hives) with or without angioedema. Affects 15-25% of the general population at some point in their lives. Acute urticaria resolves within 6 weeks; chronic urticaria persists beyond 6 weeks.

CLASSIFICATION:
- Acute Urticaria (<6 weeks): Often allergic (foods, drugs, infections)
- Chronic Spontaneous Urticaria (CSU) (>6 weeks): No identifiable trigger; autoimmune in 45% (anti-FcεRI, anti-IgE antibodies)
- Inducible/Physical Urticarias: Dermographism, cold urticaria, cholinergic, solar, pressure, aquagenic

SYMPTOMS:
- Wheals: superficial, pale-to-pink edematous plaques surrounded by erythematous flare
- Individual wheal duration <24 hours (key distinguishing feature from urticarial vasculitis)
- Intense pruritus, burning
- Angioedema: deeper dermal/subcutaneous swelling — face, lips, tongue, throat (potential airway compromise)
- No vesicles, no scaling, completely reversible

PATHOPHYSIOLOGY:
Mast cell and basophil degranulation → histamine, prostaglandins, leukotrienes release → vascular permeability, sensory nerve activation

TREATMENT:
- Second-generation H1 antihistamines (first-line): cetirizine, loratadine, fexofenadine, bilastine (up-dose up to 4x)
- H2 antihistamines adjunct: ranitidine, famotidine
- Omalizumab (anti-IgE): for CSU refractory to antihistamines — 300mg SC monthly
- Cyclosporine for severe refractory cases
- Short-course oral steroids for acute severe episodes
- Epinephrine for anaphylaxis/severe angioedema (emergency)
- Trigger avoidance (NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors worsen CSU)

ICD-10: L50.9

SKIN DISEASE: VITILIGO
=======================

OVERVIEW:
Vitiligo is an acquired autoimmune depigmentation disorder caused by selective destruction of melanocytes. Affects 0.5-2% of the global population regardless of skin type; disproportionately affects quality of life in darker-skinned individuals.

CLASSIFICATION:
- Segmental Vitiligo (SV): Unilateral, follows dermatomal distribution, stable course, poor repigmentation response
- Non-segmental Vitiligo (NSV): Bilateral, symmetric, progressive; most common
  * Generalized: Most common NSV type; acrofacial, vulgaris, mixed
  * Mucosal: Lips, genitalia
  * Universal: >80% body depigmentation

PATHOPHYSIOLOGY:
- Autoimmune T-cell mediated (CD8+ cytotoxic T cells) attack on melanocytes
- IFN-γ/CXCL10/CXCR3 axis - critical for T-cell homing to skin
- Autoantibodies to melanocyte antigens (tyrosinase, TYRP1/2)
- Oxidative stress and H2O2 accumulation in melanocytes
- Strong association with other autoimmune diseases: thyroiditis (Hashimoto's), type 1 diabetes, Addison's, alopecia areata, pernicious anemia

SYMPTOMS:
- Completely depigmented (chalk-white), well-demarcated macules and patches
- No epidermal change — skin texture and sensation normal
- Confetti-like or trichrome patterns (partial repigmentation/depigmentation)
- Koebner phenomenon present
- Leukotrichia (white hairs within lesion) — indicates follicular melanocyte loss (poor prognosis for repigmentation)
- Wood's lamp dramatically highlights lesions (milk-white fluorescence) in fair skin

AFFECTED AREAS:
- Periorificial (around eyes, mouth, nose)
- Acral (hands, feet, tips)
- Extensor surfaces, bony prominences
- Genitalia
- Areas subject to friction/pressure

TREATMENT:
Topical:
- Potent TCS (first-line active disease)
- Tacrolimus/pimecrolimus (face, genitalia, children)
- Ruxolitinib cream 1.5% (JAK1/2 inhibitor) — FDA-approved 2022 for non-segmental vitiligo ≥12yo; highest topical efficacy to date

Phototherapy:
- Narrowband UVB (most effective modality; 3x/week)
- Excimer laser 308nm (localized lesions)
- PUVA (less commonly used now)

Systemic:
- Oral minipulse dexamethasone (0.3mg/kg on 2 consecutive days/week) - arrests progression
- Afamelanotide (α-MSH analogue) + NB-UVB combination
- Oral JAK inhibitors (ruxolitinib, baricitinib) — promising clinical trials

Surgical:
- Autologous melanocyte transplant
- Blister grafting, split-thickness grafting
- ReCell spray-on skin technology
- Indicated for stable, treatment-resistant segmental vitiligo

ICD-10: L80

SKIN DISEASE: SEBORRHEIC DERMATITIS
=====================================

OVERVIEW:
Common chronic inflammatory condition affecting sebaceous gland-rich areas. Affects 1-3% of adults (up to 36% in HIV/immunocompromised). Dandruff is the mild, non-inflammatory form.

PATHOGENESIS:
- Malassezia (furfur, globosa) yeast — metabolizes sebum triglycerides into oleic acid, triggering irritant and immune response
- Sebaceous gland activity (worsens during puberty, with neurological disease)
- Abnormal immune response in susceptible individuals

SYMPTOMS:
- Greasy, yellowish scales on erythematous base
- Pruritus (variable)
- Indistinct margins (unlike psoriasis)
- Chronic, relapsing course

AFFECTED AREAS:
- Scalp (most common), glabella, eyebrows, nasolabial folds
- Ear canals, behind ears, paranasal area
- Central chest, axillae, groin (flexural involvement)

TREATMENT:
Scalp: Antifungal shampoos — ketoconazole 2%, selenium sulfide 1-2.5%, zinc pyrithione, ciclopirox 1%. Tar-based shampoos. TCS scalp solutions.
Face/Body: Ketoconazole cream 2%, ciclopirox cream, mild TCS, pimecrolimus
Severe/Resistant: Oral itraconazole pulse therapy

ICD-10: L21.9
