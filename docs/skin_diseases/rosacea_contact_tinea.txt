SKIN DISEASE: ROSACEA
======================

OVERVIEW:
Rosacea is a chronic inflammatory skin condition predominantly affecting the central face. Affects approximately 5% of the global population, with peak incidence in Fitzpatrick I-II skin types, ages 30-60. Four subtypes with distinct clinical presentations.

SUBTYPES (NRS Classification):
- Subtype 1 (ETR - Erythematotelangiectatic): Flushing, persistent central facial erythema, telangiectasias
- Subtype 2 (PPR - Papulopustular): Papules and pustules on background erythema ("acne rosacea")
- Subtype 3 (Phymatous): Skin thickening, nodularity; rhinophyma (bulbous nose) most common
- Subtype 4 (Ocular): 50% of rosacea patients; lid margin disease, conjunctivitis, episcleritis

PATHOPHYSIOLOGY:
- Dysregulation of innate immune response via TLR2 pathway
- Cathelicidin (LL-37) overproduction triggering neurogenic inflammation
- Demodex folliculorum mite colonization (10x higher in rosacea skin)
- UV-triggered vascular hyperreactivity and angiogenesis via VEGF
- Neurovascular dysregulation (explains flushing triggers)
- TRPV1 and TRPV4 receptor activation by environmental triggers

SYMPTOMS:
- Persistent centrofacial erythema and flushing
- Telangiectasias (broken blood vessels)
- Papules and pustules (resemble acne but without comedones)
- Burning and stinging sensation
- Skin sensitivity to topical products
- Ocular symptoms: gritty eyes, blurred vision, photophobia
- Rhinophyma (bulbous, porous nose in severe cases)
- Lymphedema of cheeks in severe disease

TRIGGERS:
- Sun exposure and heat
- Spicy foods, hot beverages, alcohol (especially red wine)
- Extreme temperatures, wind
- Exercise
- Stress and anxiety
- Certain skincare products (alcohol, witch hazel, fragrance)
- Medications: topical vasodilators
- Menopause/hormonal changes

TREATMENT:
Topical: metronidazole 0.75-1%, azelaic acid 15-20%, ivermectin 1% (targets Demodex), brimonidine 0.33% gel (vasoconstrictor for erythema), oxymetazoline 1% cream
Systemic: doxycycline 40mg modified-release (sub-antimicrobial dose), isotretinoin (severe/refractory)
Procedural: vascular lasers (PDL 595nm, Nd:YAG), IPL for telangiectasias, surgical debulking for rhinophyma
Ocular: lid hygiene, azithromycin eye drops, cyclosporine 0.05% ophthalmic

ICD-10: L71.9

SKIN DISEASE: CONTACT DERMATITIS
==================================

OVERVIEW:
Inflammatory skin reaction caused by direct contact with an external agent. Two main types with distinct immunological mechanisms.

TYPES:
1. Irritant Contact Dermatitis (ICD) - 80% of cases
   - Non-immunological, dose-dependent cytotoxic reaction
   - Common irritants: soaps, detergents, solvents, acids, alkalis, prolonged water exposure
   - "Housewife's eczema" - chronic hand dermatitis from repeated wet work

2. Allergic Contact Dermatitis (ACD) - 20% of cases
   - Type IV delayed hypersensitivity (sensitization then elicitation)
   - Common allergens: nickel (most common), fragrances, preservatives (MI/MCI), rubber chemicals, hair dye (PPD), poison ivy/oak (urushiol), cobalt, chromate
   - Requires prior sensitization (days to years)

SYMPTOMS:
Acute: erythema, edema, vesicles, oozing, pruritus at contact site
Chronic: lichenification, scaling, fissuring, hyperpigmentation
ACD: lesions may spread beyond contact area (auto-sensitization); sharp geometric patterns suggest external cause

DIAGNOSIS:
- Patch testing (gold standard for ACD) - TRUE test or extended series
- History and pattern recognition (distribution matches contact pattern)

TREATMENT:
- Identify and avoid causative agent
- Barrier emollients, gloves for hand protection
- Topical corticosteroids (mild-potent depending on site)
- Tacrolimus/pimecrolimus for face/flexures
- Systemic corticosteroids for extensive/severe cases
- Antihistamines for pruritus

ICD-10: L23 (ACD), L24 (ICD)

SKIN DISEASE: TINEA (DERMATOPHYTOSIS / RINGWORM)
==================================================

OVERVIEW:
Fungal infections caused by dermatophytes (Trichophyton, Microsporum, Epidermophyton species). Named by anatomical location. Very common — tinea pedis affects 15% of global population.

TYPES:
- Tinea Capitis: Scalp; kerion (inflammatory), gray patch, black dot presentations; primarily in children
- Tinea Corporis (Ringworm): Body; classic annular plaque with central clearing and advancing scaly border
- Tinea Pedis (Athlete's Foot): Interdigital maceration, plantar scaling, bullous type
- Tinea Cruris (Jock Itch): Groin, inner thighs; well-demarcated erythematous plaques
- Tinea Unguium (Onychomycosis): Nails; distal-lateral subungual most common; opacification, thickening, onycholysis
- Tinea Versicolor (Pityriasis versicolor - actually Malassezia, not dermatophyte): Hypo/hyperpigmented macules, trunk, responds to UV

DIAGNOSIS:
- KOH preparation: branching hyphae (septate) with arthrospores
- Wood's lamp: M. canis fluoresces green
- Fungal culture: Sabouraud dextrose agar
- Dermoscopy

TREATMENT:
Topical: clotrimazole, terbinafine, miconazole, ketoconazole (body/feet/groin — 2-4 weeks)
Systemic (extensive/scalp/nails): 
- Terbinafine (nail: 250mg/day × 6 weeks fingers, 12 weeks toes)
- Itraconazole pulse therapy
- Griseofulvin (tinea capitis in children)
- Fluconazole

ICD-10: B35.0-B35.9
