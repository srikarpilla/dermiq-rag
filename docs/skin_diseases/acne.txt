SKIN DISEASE: ACNE VULGARIS
=====================================

OVERVIEW:
Acne vulgaris is a chronic inflammatory disease of the pilosebaceous unit, representing the most prevalent skin disorder globally. It affects approximately 85% of individuals between ages 12-24, though adult-onset acne is increasingly common, particularly in women aged 25-40.

CLASSIFICATION:
- Grade I (Comedonal): Primarily open (blackheads) and closed (whiteheads) comedones without significant inflammation
- Grade II (Mild Inflammatory): Scattered papules and pustules with fewer than 20 comedones
- Grade III (Moderate): Multiple papules/pustules, possible nodule formation, moderate to severe comedones (20-100)
- Grade IV (Severe/Nodulo-cystic): Extensive nodules, cysts, potential scarring, greater than 5 pseudocysts

PATHOPHYSIOLOGY:
Four primary mechanisms drive acne development:
1. Follicular hyperkeratinization - Abnormal desquamation of follicular epithelium leads to microcomedone formation
2. Sebaceous gland hyperactivity - Androgens (testosterone, DHEA-S) stimulate excess sebum production
3. Cutibacterium acnes proliferation - This anaerobic gram-positive bacterium thrives in sebum-rich environments
4. Immune-mediated inflammation - TLR2 activation triggers IL-1, IL-8, TNF-alpha release

SYMPTOMS:
- Open comedones (blackheads) - dilated follicles with oxidized melanin
- Closed comedones (whiteheads) - flesh-colored dome-shaped papules
- Papules (< 5mm inflammatory lesions)
- Pustules (papules with visible purulent material)
- Nodules (> 5mm, deep, painful)
- Cysts (fluctuant, pus-filled, risk of scarring)
- Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH)
- Atrophic or hypertrophic scarring

AFFECTED AREAS:
Face (forehead, nose, chin - T-zone), chest, back, shoulders, upper arms

TRIGGERS & RISK FACTORS:
- Hormonal fluctuations (puberty, menstrual cycle, PCOS, pregnancy)
- Comedogenic cosmetics and sunscreens
- High glycemic index diet and dairy consumption
- Psychological stress (increases CRH, triggering sebum production)
- Certain medications: corticosteroids, lithium, isoniazid, B vitamins
- Friction/pressure (acne mechanica) from helmets, straps, phones
- Genetic predisposition (concordance rate ~80% in twins)

DIAGNOSIS:
- Clinical examination is primary diagnostic method
- Hormonal panel if endocrine etiology suspected (LH/FSH ratio, DHEA-S, free testosterone)
- Culture if antibiotic resistance suspected
- Consider PCOS workup in women with truncal acne and menstrual irregularities

TREATMENT PROTOCOLS:
Topical Treatments:
- Benzoyl peroxide (2.5-10%) - bactericidal, reduces resistance
- Retinoids: tretinoin (0.025-0.1%), adapalene (0.1-0.3%), tazarotene
- Salicylic acid (0.5-2%) - comedolytic, anti-inflammatory
- Niacinamide (4-5%) - reduces sebum, anti-inflammatory
- Azelaic acid (15-20%) - antibacterial, reduces PIH
- Topical antibiotics: clindamycin, erythromycin (always with BPO)
- Dapsone gel 5-7.5%

Systemic Treatments:
- Oral antibiotics: doxycycline (50-100mg), minocycline, tetracycline (6-12 weeks)
- Combined oral contraceptives (estrogen-progestin) for females
- Spironolactone (50-200mg) for hormonal acne in women
- Isotretinoin (0.5-1mg/kg/day) for severe/recalcitrant acne - requires iPLEDGE monitoring

Procedural Treatments:
- Chemical peels (glycolic acid, salicylic acid, Jessner's)
- Comedone extraction
- Intralesional corticosteroid injections for nodules/cysts
- Laser and light therapies (PDT, blue light, 1450nm diode)
- Microneedling for scarring

PREVENTION:
- Non-comedogenic skincare and cosmetics
- Gentle cleansing twice daily
- Avoid manual extraction
- SPF 30+ (non-comedogenic)
- Dietary modifications: reduce high-GI foods and skim dairy

PROGNOSIS:
Most cases resolve by mid-20s. Severe nodulo-cystic acne risks permanent scarring without adequate treatment. PIH can persist for months to years, particularly in darker skin phototypes (Fitzpatrick III-VI).

ICD-10 CODE: L70.0 (Acne vulgaris)
